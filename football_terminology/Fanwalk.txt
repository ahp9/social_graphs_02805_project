'''Fanwalk''' or '''fan walk''', is the practice of [[association football]] fans walking as a large group to and from the stadium.<ref name=":0">{{Cite journal |last=Brechbühl |first=Alain |date=2017 |title=Escalation vs. non-escalation of fan violence in football? Narratives from ultra fans, police officers and security employees |url=https://dx.doi.org/10.1080/17430437.2016.1221932 |journal=Sport in Society |volume=20 |issue=7 |pages=861–879 |doi=10.1080/17430437.2016.1221932 |via=EBSCOHost|url-access=subscription }}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=Fan-Walk zum Stadion in Dortmund |url=http://www.dfb.de/news/detail/fan-walk-zum-stadion-in-dortmund-262750/ |access-date=2024-07-02 |website=DFB - Deutscher Fußball-Bund e.V. |language=de}}</ref> In European football, they are often escorted by police and security personnel to prevent violent incidents involving [[ultras]].<ref name=":0" /> Dutch football fans are particularly well known for their fanwalks.<ref name=":3" /><ref>{{Cite news |date=16 June 2024 |title=Mann droht Polizei mit Spitzhacke – Beamte schießen |url=https://www.proquest.com/docview/3068593112 |access-date=2024-07-03 |work=Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung (online) |id={{ProQuest|3068593112}} |language=de }}</ref><ref>{{Cite news |last=Lee |first=Sophie |date=1 August 2022 |title=Let's reap the high profile |url=https://www.proquest.com/docview/2696786952 |access-date=2024-07-03 |work=The Press and Journal |id={{ProQuest|2696786952}} }}</ref> Notable examples at [[UEFA Euro 2024|Euro 2024]] include the fanwalk by supporters of the [[Netherlands national football team]] in Leipzig, which drew between 20,000 and 25,000 fans wearing orange jerseys and hats.<ref name=":3">{{Cite news |date=22 June 2024 |title=Polizei begeistert vom Fanwalk der Niederländer in Leipzig |url=https://www.saechsische.de/leipzig/leipzig-fussball-em-fanwalk-niederlaender-polizei-beeindruckt-positiv-6015348.html |access-date=2024-07-03 |work=Sächsische.de |publisher=[[Sächsische Zeitung]] |language=de}}</ref>

The concept of the fanwalk became popular during the [[2010 FIFA World Cup]] in South Africa.<ref name=":1">{{Cite book |title=Heritage, Culture, and Politics in the Postcolony |date=2012 |publisher=Columbia University Press |location=New York; Chichester; West Sussex |pages=80–134 |chapter=Four. Monument, Ruin, and Redress in South African Heritage |doi=10.7312/herw16018-005 |isbn=978-0-231-53072-9 }}</ref><ref name=":2">{{Cite web |date=18 August 2010 |title=Cape Town Stadium is Hosting a Soccer Event on Friday 27 August |url=https://www.westerncape.gov.za/news/cape-town-stadium-hosting-soccer-event-friday-27-august |access-date=2024-07-03 |website=Western Cape Government}}</ref> The pedestrian route from central [[Cape Town]] to the [[Cape Town Stadium]] at [[Green Point, Cape Town|Green Point]], referred to as the Fanwalk, was carefully designed to promote local businesses while promoting crowd safety and creating a festive atmosphere.<ref name=":1" /><ref name=":2" /> An estimated 580,000 people experienced the fan walk at the 2010 World Cup.<ref name=":2" />
