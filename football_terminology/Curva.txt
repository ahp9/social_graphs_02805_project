'''Curva''' {{IPA|it|ˈkurva|}} ([[plural]]: ''curve'' {{IPA|it|ˈkurve|}}) is an [[Italian language|Italian]] term or name for curved stands of seating located at sports [[Stadium|stadiums]], particularly in Italy; so named, originally, due to their curved or bending shape. The curva plays an integral part in the culture of [[Ultras#Characteristics|Ultras]] and European [[Association football|football]].

==Composition==
[[File:San Siro Stadium, Inter fans Derby - 2009.jpg|thumb|[[Inter Milan]] Fans in Curva Nord Milano]]
The majority of stands referred to as a "curva" are located behind the goals in their respective stadiums and contain the most vocal supporters within them, often known as [[Ultras]]. They are usually curved in shape, in some form whether minor or major, often due to the presence of a running track around the pitch.<ref>{{cite web
  | url=http://www.italyheaven.co.uk/rome/football.html
  | title=Football in Rome | publisher=Italy Heaven
  | access-date=9 July 2009 }}
</ref> The [[San Siro|Stadio Giuseppe Meazza]] provides an example of two prominent stands referred to as "curva", "[[F.C. Internazionale Milano#Supporters and rivalries|Curva Nord]]" and "[[A.C. Milan#Stadium|Curva Sud]]", which contain only minor curves at their corners, while the [[Stadio Olimpico]] provides an example of two completely curved stands. A curva may extend from one corner flag to another or be located centrally behind the goal, bordered by two separate corner sections for ticketing or organizational purposes.<ref>{{cite web
  | url=http://www.asromaultras.it/stadioolimpico.html
  | title=LO STADIO OLIMPICO
  | access-date=9 July 2009
  | url-status=dead
  | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090806090249/http://www.asromaultras.it/stadioolimpico.html
  | archive-date=6 August 2009
  }}
</ref>

==Significance==
[[File:OlimpicoRoma.JPG|right|thumb|A.S. Roma Fans in Curva Sud]]
Being home to the most openly passionate supporters in a stadium, the curva is usually the focal point of a club's support. It is often the scene of dramatic [[Tifo|choreographed displays]] of support and occasionally, disapproval for a team or club.<ref>{{cite web | url=http://archives.tcm.ie/irishexaminer/2004/01/07/story931987514.asp | title=Soccer: Roma Therapy | publisher=[[Irish Examiner]] | author=Micheal Lehane | date=7 January 2004 | access-date=9 July 2009 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090709053906/http://archives.tcm.ie/irishexaminer/2004/01/07/story931987514.asp | archive-date=9 July 2009 | url-status=dead }}</ref> These displays often take on an importance of their own, particularly in games involving [[Major football rivalries|rivals]] where both sets of supporters aim to outdo each other.<ref name="Il Derby della Capitale">{{cite news
  |url=http://www.cbc.ca/sports/indepth/feature-soccer-rome.html
  | title=Il Derby della Capitale | publisher=CBC News
  | access-date=9 July 2009 }}
</ref> In certain countries, particularly those where sports clubs and supporters are extremely reflective of the local culture, the curva can become quite politicised in nature.<ref name="Il Derby della Capitale" /> Therefore, it is not uncommon for a curva to be split into individual factions or groups, either solely or partially, based on politics, and for one group to hold significant control of the curva and its inhabitants on match days. [[Ultras]] groups within a curva often benefit from this degree of relative uniformity, when there are issues regarding supporters' rights and the [[commercialisation]] of sports and [[Association football|football]] in particular.
